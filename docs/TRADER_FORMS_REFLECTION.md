# REFLECTION — Rigorously testing new trade-FORMS inside the anti-overfitting framework

*Three trader-style fronts (NF1 support/resistance, NF2 target+stop-loss brackets, NF3 low-N confluence) run through the committed gate stack, realistic cost, and a surrogate/placebo control. All three KILL. This note reflects on what the gates capture vs. miss for these forms, and the deeper reasons the kills were inevitable.*

---

## Executive summary

We tested three *forms* of trading that are categorically different from the indicator/feature strategies the project killed previously: **level-based discretionary techniques** (S/R, pivots, Fibonacci, Donchian, round numbers), **path-dependent exits** (intrabar TP/SL brackets), and **rare pre-registered confluence**. The point was not "find more indicators" but to probe whether the gate stack generalizes to trade-FORMS where the edge claim is about *price geometry*, *exit management*, or *signal alignment* rather than a feature's predictive sign. It does — but each form forced an explicit methodological upgrade to make the test honest:

- **Path-dependent exits (NF2)** required a *per-trade P&L series* (not position×next-return) plus a **bracket-on-surrogate null** — apply the identical bracket to driftless/phase-randomized paths and ask whether the bracket *reshapes noise into apparent edge*. It does reshape (win-rate 33%→65%, skew −0.64→+2.22) but the reshaping is the same on real and surrogate paths.
- **Low-N confluence (NF3)** required genuine **pre-registration** — one rule, one statistic, every threshold counted in N — so the honest N stays small (N=8) and the deflation bar stays low. But the low bar bought nothing: there was no edge to clear even a low bar, and the rarity-vs-edge separation the hypothesis needed does not exist.
- **Level/discretionary techniques (NF1)** required **counting the level-construction in N** (168 configs: level-type × rule × parameter × variant), so that "I just drew the obvious levels" cannot smuggle in a free search. With construction counted and a surrogate that preserves vol+autocorrelation, the apparent S/R edge is revealed as filtered beta (placeboP=0.609).

The unifying result is the project's central thesis restated for trader forms: **none of these techniques manufactures edge.** Geometry is decoration on trend; exit management moves variance/skew around a fixed expectancy; confluence is either a long-biased trend filter or too rare to validate. Each KILL is one more falsified easy-money technique — publishable evidence, not a null result to be embarrassed about.

## 1. Results summary — one line each

- **NF1 — Support/Resistance & price-level techniques:** **KILL** (surrogate, placeboP=0.609). Every winner is a *breakout/breakout-retest* momentum variant riding trend; phase/block/cross-sectional surrogates that destroy genuine level structure score equal-or-better, so the "edge" is filtered beta, not the levels.
- **NF2 — Target + Stop-Loss professional brackets:** **KILL** (net_of_cost, 0/47 pass). Brackets *reshape* the P&L distribution dramatically but do not move the ≈0 gross expectancy; the only place real beats the surrogate null (a ~0.03–0.04 gross-Sharpe path-selective-exit effect) is fully eaten by 8 bps round-trip.
- **NF3 — Confluence of rare pre-registered signals:** **KILL** (baselines, then DSR/surrogate/holdout). Pre-registration genuinely lowered the bar (N=8, expectedMaxSharpe 0.049) but there is *no* threshold k that is simultaneously rare AND edgeful — loosen k → long-biased trend filter beaten by random-lottery; tighten k → 2 trades, indistinguishable from luck.

## 2. What the gates CAPTURE vs. MISS for trader-style forms — and the principled protocol

The committed stack (net-of-cost → baselines → Deflated Sharpe@true-N → CPCV/PBO → Harvey–Liu haircut → surrogate/placebo → consume-once holdout) was built around *position-series* strategies. Trader forms have three features that the vanilla stack does **not** automatically see. For each, the gate that would otherwise be blind, and the protocol that restores honesty:

### (a) Path-dependent exits (TP/SL brackets) — the surrogate must be a *bracket-on-surrogate* null

**What the gates miss by default:** A vanilla surrogate randomizes a *return/position series*. But a TP/SL strategy is not a position series — it is a **per-trade P&L series** generated by walking each bar's intrabar HIGH/LOW. A bracket is a nonlinear functional of the *path*, and a nonlinear functional can *reshape noise into a heavy-tailed, high-win-rate distribution that superficially looks like edge*. If you only test the bracket on real data, you cannot tell whether the nice-looking distribution is the entry's information or the bracket's geometry acting on any path.

**Principled protocol (what NF2 did):**
1. Generate a genuine **per-trade P&L series** by resolving TP/SL intrabar on the real HIGH/LOW path (conservative stop-first tie-break when both fall in one bar — pessimistic by design, no look-ahead: enter at signal close, start the exit walk next bar).
2. Build a **bracket-on-surrogate null**: apply the *identical* bracket machinery to (i) driftless GBM paths and (ii) phase-randomized paths whose intrabar range is reconstructed from the real bars. Ask: *does the bracket reshape noise into the same apparent edge?*
3. Compare real-bracket Sharpe to the surrogate-bracket null (permutation p-value), and separately run an **attribution control** — apply the surrogate test to the *un-bracketed* fixed-horizon hold to isolate whether any gap is entry alpha or a pure exit-geometry effect.
4. Charge 8 bps round-trip on every trade's entry+exit; report turnover.

NF2's null came back indistinguishable (gbm −0.0948, phase −0.0906 vs. real-breakout −0.0795), the attribution showed the entry itself is noise (placeboP 0.10–0.35), and the lone real-beats-null effect (trailing/far-target picking up intrabar autocorrelation, ~0.03–0.04 gross) died at net_of_cost. Without the bracket-on-surrogate null, the +2.22-skew, 65%-win-rate trailing stop would have *looked* like a discovery.

### (b) Low-N confluence — pre-registration is the gate, and you must pay the trade-count-variance tax

**What the gates miss by default:** Deflated Sharpe and the haircut deflate by N. The temptation with "rare confluence" is to claim N=1 ("I only tested one rule") after having quietly searched signal definitions, thresholds, and combinations — which makes the deflation bar fraudulently low. Conversely, if you honestly pre-register, the *small* N gives you a low bar, but rarity means *few joint occurrences*, so the Sharpe itself has huge sampling variance — the strategy can clear DSR by luck precisely because there are too few trades.

**Principled protocol (what NF3 did):**
1. **Pre-register one decision rule and one statistic** *before looking* (Sharpe; "go long next day when ≥k signals fire"). Commit the signal definitions; do not search them.
2. **Count every variant in N** — every k and every committed threshold variant — and feed that honest N (here 8) to DSR and the haircut. Do not pretend N=1; pre-registration earns a *small* N, not N=1.
3. Add a **search-level placebo**: run the *entire* 6-signal + grid-sweep + best-of-8 machinery on phase/block-randomized price paths and compare the real best-of-8 to that distribution (real 0.0435 sat inside, p=0.383).
4. Confront the **rarity/variance tradeoff explicitly**: report the trade-count at every k. NF3's finding *is* the tradeoff — k=3 fires 735/1082 days (not rare, just long-bias) and loses to random-lottery; k=6 fires 2 days (degenerate, un-validatable). Report the degenerate corner rather than hiding it.

The honest N (8) lowered the bar; the form still killed, because pre-registration cannot manufacture an edge the aligned signals do not contain.

### (c) Discretionary / level-based techniques — the level-construction must be counted in N

**What the gates miss by default:** A discretionary trader "draws the obvious support line." That hand is an *unpriced search*: choosing level-type (pivot vs. Fibonacci vs. Donchian vs. round number), proximity bands, lookbacks, and the bounce-vs-breakout rule is exactly the multiple-comparisons surface DSR exists to deflate. If you only validate the *one* level rule you ended up liking, N=1 hides the search and DSR is toothless.

**Principled protocol (what NF1 did):**
1. **Enumerate the construction as a grid** and count it in N: 5 level-types × 3 rules × parameter grids × 2 variants = **168 configs**, fed verbatim to DSR (expMaxSharpe 0.021) and the haircut.
2. Carve the **consume-once holdout FIRST**, before selection, so the vault is untouched by the level search.
3. Run a **structure-destroying surrogate**: phase-randomized + block-bootstrap + cross-sectional surrogates preserve volatility and autocorrelation but destroy genuine *level* structure. If the level edge is real, the surrogate (which has no levels) should score worse. It did not — placeboP=0.609, real Sharpe sits in the middle of the null — proving the "S/R edge" is the preserved trend/beta, not the levels.

Counting the construction is what turned "Bollinger-edge breakout-retest works (ann Sharpe 1.07)" into the correct verdict: it passed five gates and the holdout, then the surrogate showed the levels carry no information the null can't reproduce.

## 3. The deeper point — TP/SL and the fair-game theorem

For a **driftless / no-edge entry**, the stopped/targeted process is a martingale, and by the **optional stopping theorem** the expected P&L at any stopping rule (any TP/SL placement, trailing stop, R-multiple scale-out) is **zero** — independent of where you put the bracket. Stops and targets are *stopping times*; they cannot change the expectation of a fair game. What they **can** change is the *shape* of the terminal distribution: a tight stop / far target gives a low win-rate, positive-skew lottery; a far stop / near target gives a high win-rate, negative-skew "picking up pennies" profile. **Same mean, different variance and skew.**

This is exactly what **NF2 measured on real BTC**: across brackets the win-rate swept 33%→65% and per-trade skew swept −0.64 (2:1 target) to +2.22 (trailing), while **gross mean expectancy stayed ≈0/negative**. The reshaping is dramatic and real; the expectation does not move right. Therefore:

> **"Risk management" manages variance, it does not manufacture edge.** A stop does not make a coin-flip profitable; it only decides whether you lose in many small bites or few large ones. **Only the ENTRY signal's edge matters** — the exit can shape how that edge (or its absence) is delivered, but it cannot create a positive expectation where the entry has none.

NF2 confirmed the corollary directly: the **un-bracketed breakout entry is itself indistinguishable from the surrogate null** (placeboP 0.10–0.35). With zero entry edge, the fair-game theorem guarantees no bracket can rescue it — and none did (0/47 net of cost). The one whisper of real signal (trailing/far-target capturing genuine intrabar path autocorrelation, ~0.03–0.04 gross Sharpe) is a *path-selective-exit* effect, not entry alpha, and it is sub-economic: 8 bps round-trip eats it whole. The framework was sensitive enough to *detect* the tiny genuine effect and disciplined enough to *refuse to promote it* because it does not survive cost. That is the theorem made operational.

## 4. The low-N tension — and whether NF3 found the sweet spot

Pre-registration creates a genuine tension:

- **Pre-registration LOWERS the deflation bar.** A small honest N means DSR's expectedMaxSharpe is tiny (NF3: N=8 → expMaxSharpe 0.049). A modest real edge clears a low bar.
- **But rarity RAISES sampling variance.** Few joint occurrences mean the Sharpe estimate itself is noisy; a rare rule can clear DSR *by luck* precisely because it has too few trades to pin down. Force rarity hard enough (NF3 k=6) and you get 2 trades — un-validatable.

The honest **sweet spot** is therefore a **small set of STRONG, PRE-COMMITTED, economically-distinct signals with enough JOINT occurrences to clear a consume-once holdout** — small enough N to keep the deflation bar low, distinct enough economics that the signals are not collinear restatements of one factor, and frequent enough in their conjunction that the holdout has statistical power.

**Did NF3 find that sweet spot? No — and the failure is structural, not a tuning miss.** NF3 pre-registered six economically-distinct, causally-computed signals (carry, basis/contango, oversold-at-support, trend, low cross-sectional dispersion, momentum) — exactly the right *kind* of inputs. But there is **no k that is simultaneously rare AND edgeful**:

- **Loosen k (k=3):** 735/1082 trade-days. Not "rare confluence" at all — just a long-biased trend filter that inherits BTC's bull drift, and it **loses to the random-lottery baseline** (picking the same number of days at random does as well).
- **Tighten k (k=6):** 2 trade-days. Statistically degenerate; cannot be distinguished from luck.

So **rarity and edge were not separable in this signal set** — the regime the hypothesis hoped for (rare *and* informative joint firings) does not exist here. The low honest N helped the *statistics* (low bar, search-level placebo p=0.383, internal surrogate placeboP=0.744) but could not *manufacture* an edge the aligned signals do not contain. The consume-once holdout was negative (−0.090), which is the decisive, leak-proof confirmation. NF3 is the clean demonstration of why the sweet spot is *narrow*: getting all three properties (low N, distinct economics, sufficient joint frequency) to hold at once is hard, and when they don't, the form kills honestly.

## 5. How this fits the project's falsification philosophy

This project is a **falsification engine**, not an alpha hunt. Its prior is KILL (26/28 before these three; now 29/31), and that prior is a *feature*: the gate stack is calibrated so that the overwhelmingly likely outcome for any easy-money technique is rejection, and a SURVIVE would be a genuinely rare, load-bearing finding.

Each of these three KILLs is **publishable evidence that a specific, widely-believed trader technique does not produce a real, cost-surviving, out-of-sample edge:**

- **NF1** falsifies the claim that *price levels* (S/R, pivots, Fibonacci, round numbers, Bollinger edges) carry exploitable information — what looks like an S/R edge is filtered beta a structure-destroying surrogate reproduces exactly.
- **NF2** falsifies the claim that *target/stop-loss "risk management" creates edge* — it reshapes variance/skew around a fixed expectancy, and the fair-game theorem says it must.
- **NF3** falsifies the claim that *stacking rare signals into confluence* yields a tradeable rule — rarity and edge are not separable; you get a trend filter or a degenerate corner.

These are not failures of the experiment; they are the experiment **working**. A clean KILL — run on real data, through the committed gates, with realistic cost and a surrogate that the strategy must *beat*, recorded with honest N and honest caveats — is a falsifiable, reproducible result that contributes exactly the kind of negative evidence a credible public research project should publish. The methodological upgrades these forms forced (per-trade P&L + bracket-on-surrogate null; pre-registration with N-honesty; level-construction counted in N) now make the framework strictly stronger for the *next* trade-form we test. **The honest answer to "how do we rigorously test new trade-forms?" is: build the form's own structure-destroying null, count its construction in N, charge it real cost, and let the consume-once holdout have the last word — then publish the kill.**
